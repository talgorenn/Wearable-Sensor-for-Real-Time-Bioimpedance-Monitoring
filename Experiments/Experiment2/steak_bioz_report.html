01 — Background

Experiment Overview

Bioimpedance (BioZ) measures the electrical impedance of biological tissue by injecting a small AC current and measuring the resulting voltage. The complex impedance Z varies with frequency due to the capacitive behavior of cell membranes and the resistive properties of intra- and extracellular fluids.

This experiment measures a single piece of raw steak tissue with the MAX30001G analog front-end across a full 6-point log-spaced frequency sweep (125 Hz – 128 kHz), then injects 1 mL of saline solution directly into the tissue near the electrodes and repeats the identical sweep. Comparing the two spectra isolates the effect of the added ionic conductivity on the impedance response, extending the single-frequency sensitivity check (see the sensitivity experiment) to the full spectrum.

02 — Setup

Hardware Configuration

The MAX30001G EV Kit was configured in bipolar 2-electrode mode: both sense (BIP/BIN) and drive (DRVP/DRVN) electrodes share the same physical contacts via the DP_BP and DN_BN jumpers. ECG electrodes were inserted directly into the steak tissue for stable contact. The BioZ channel used a modulation phase offset of 0° for all frequencies, chopped drive with resistive common-mode, low drive current (110/220 nA), 32 sps sample rate, and a bypassed (DC) digital high-pass filter.

Each frequency was recorded for roughly 20 seconds. After the full baseline sweep, 1 mL of saline solution was injected into the tissue near the electrode contacts and the identical 6-point sweep was repeated.

Note: Bipolar measurements include electrode contact impedance in the total reading. A tetrapolar (4-electrode) setup would isolate tissue impedance alone. Despite this, the relative difference before vs. after saline injection is still clearly measurable.

03 — Results

Cole Model Fit & Nyquist Reconstruction

The MAX30001G outputs |Z| magnitude only — phase is not measured. The Cole model Z(ω) = R∞ + (R₀ − R∞) / (1 + (jωτ)^α) was fitted to each magnitude spectrum (before and after saline) via nonlinear least-squares. The fitted parameters were then used to reconstruct the complex impedance and produce the Nyquist (Re[Z] vs. −Im[Z]) and Bode magnitude plots below.

Nyquist plot (left) and Bode magnitude (right) — before vs. after saline, overlaid. Labeled points are the fitted model evaluated at round frequencies, not raw samples.

Condition R₀ (Ω) R∞ (Ω) α (−) Diameter R₀−R∞ (Ω)
Before Saline 340 250 0.82 90
After Saline 180 130 0.82 50
Change in diameter: 90 Ω → 50 Ω (−40 Ω, −44%). After saline injection the semicircle diameter shrank by roughly 44%, indicating increased tissue conductivity (lower overall impedance) at both the low- and high-frequency limits.
04 — Conclusions

Discussion & Takeaways

The experiment demonstrates that the MAX30001G EV Kit can detect a 1 mL saline injection into steak tissue as a clear, spectrum-wide shift in impedance, well-captured by the Cole model fit despite only magnitude data being available.

Saline lowers both R₀ and R∞: R₀ dropped 47% (340 → 180 Ω) and R∞ dropped 48% (250 → 130 Ω) — a near-uniform reduction across the spectrum, consistent with the injected saline adding a strongly conductive pathway that is not confined to one frequency band.
Cole arc shrinks proportionally: The semicircle diameter (R₀ − R∞) contracted from 90 Ω to 50 Ω (−44%) alongside the overall impedance drop, rather than stretching or distorting — the shape of the dispersion is preserved, just rescaled.
Dispersion exponent unchanged: α remained at 0.82 before and after, indicating the relative distribution of relaxation times in the tissue was not altered by the saline injection — only the resistive scale (R₀, R∞) changed.
Limitation — magnitude-only fitting: The MAX30001G outputs |Z| only, not phase. Cole-Cole reconstruction is estimated from model fitting rather than direct measurement. A device with phase output (e.g. MAX30009) would give a more accurate result.
